# Results

Across 89,850 analysed 100 km² areas and 3,307,653 Pareto solutions, only 49,088 areas (54.63%) admitted at least one feasible solution with a risk level change rate of at least 0.35, indicating that a 35% reduction target is not universally achievable under the current decision bounds and cost assumptions. The policy-screening landscape (Fig. 1a) shows a clear escalation from cost-efficiency-first to maximum mitigation in both expenditure and achieved reduction. Maximum mitigation yielded the highest mean risk level change rate (0.512) and the lowest mean optimised risk (0.081), but it also imposed the largest **Mean cost per 100 km² in 20 years** (US$212.25 million). By contrast, target-first attained the same overall threshold-reaching area fraction as maximum mitigation (54.63%) at a substantially lower mean cost (US$92.88 million), corresponding to a 56.24% reduction in mean expenditure.

The full area-level distributions clarify why these aggregate differences emerge (Fig. 1b). Maximum mitigation shifts the entire distribution of risk level change rate upward, whereas cost-efficiency-first is strongly concentrated near small improvements, with much of its density below the threshold of 0.35. Balanced compromise occupies an intermediate position, while target-first is concentrated around the threshold itself, reflecting its rule-based selection of the least-cost feasible solution that still satisfies the target whenever such a solution exists.

The threshold-reaching intersection structure further shows that the four rules do not simply differ in magnitude, but also in the sets of 100 km² areas they effectively serve (Fig. 1c). Of all analysed areas, 31.31% reached the threshold under all four rules, whereas 18.74% reached it under target-first, maximum mitigation and balanced compromise but not under cost-efficiency-first. A further 3.99% reached the threshold only under target-first and maximum mitigation, and only 0.59% did so under target-first, maximum mitigation and cost-efficiency-first but not under balanced compromise. Importantly, target-first and maximum mitigation shared the same attainable area set, implying that the major difference between them lies not in where the target can be reached, but in how aggressively they move along the attainable Pareto frontier once such areas are identified.

The ternary cost-composition view (Fig. 1d) indicates that the strategies also differ mechanistically in how expenditure is allocated. Cost-efficiency-first is overwhelmingly LAI-dominated, with LAI accounting for 91.66% of its mean cost, whereas maximum mitigation shifts strongly toward WTD expenditure (47.32% share). Target-first and balanced compromise occupy mixed positions in the ISA–WTD–LAI space, indicating that practical target satisfaction often requires coordinated intervention rather than reliance on a single component.

Provincial heterogeneity remains pronounced (Supplementary Fig. 1). Under target-first screening, threshold-reaching area fractions are highest in Beijing (95.70%), Jilin (88.28%) and Guizhou (85.54%), but lowest in Hainan (21.35%), Tibet (21.91%) and Qinghai (32.45%). This strong spatial heterogeneity supports the view that nationally uniform implementation is unlikely to be optimal and motivates subsequent comparisons among province-level, zone-specific and locally adaptive governance strategies.
