# 2.1 Probability distribution of sinkhole collapses

The national susceptibility to sinkhole collapse in China exhibits pronounced spatial clustering and is supported by a calibrated, repeatedly validated probability model, providing a quantitative basis for prioritizing risk mitigation. Our five-tier classification scheme delineates a broad corridor of elevated occurrence probability spanning central and eastern China, contrasting sharply with the predominantly low-risk domains in the west. This spatial pattern should therefore be interpreted not as a raw inventory density map, but as a model-derived probability surface constrained by balanced historical samples and probability calibration. The spatial concentration is quantitatively robust: Shanxi Province alone harbors approximately 1,017 km² of the highest-risk terrain, accounting for 40.5% of its total area. Similarly, Guangxi, Hunan, Jiangxi, and Guizhou encompass 724, 563, 505, and 337 km² of extreme-risk areas, representing proportional shares of 25.6%, 21.3%, 23.2%, and 14.6%, respectively. Collectively, these top five provinces comprise roughly 59% of the nation's highest-risk footprint, a figure that escalates to 79% when considering the top ten. Notably, high-resolution mapping within these macro-hotspots exposes coherent, localized high-susceptibility clusters demarcated by abrupt transitions to lower-risk zones.

Further deconstructing this spatial footprint underscores the severe provincial concentration of sinkhole hazards, juxtaposed with pronounced fine-scale heterogeneity. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, while central, eastern, and southern China host the primary high-susceptibility clusters, the western regions remain largely unaffected. A detailed provincial breakdown in Fig. 1c confirms Shanxi's leading position, followed closely by a secondary tier comprising Guangxi, Hunan, and Jiangxi. A third tier encompasses Guizhou and Shandong (285 km²; 11.3%), while Liaoning, Guangdong, and Hubei exhibit the highest-risk extents (205–234 km², with shares ranging from 7.6% to 10.1%). Conversely, hazard exposure diminishes rapidly in Hebei (154 km²; 4.3%), falling below 50 km² in provinces like Zhejiang and Jiangsu, and approaching negligible levels near 15 km² in municipalities such as Beijing, Tianjin, and Shanghai, as well as in Hainan and Sichuan. In absolute terms, the top five and top ten provinces account for 3,146 km² and 4,244 km² of the national highest-risk area, respectively. Crucially, regional magnifications in Fig. 1b reveal that within dominant provinces like Shanxi and Guangxi, these critical risk zones do not manifest randomly; rather, they form continuous, morphologically coherent belts and blocks deeply embedded within broader high-sensitivity geologic domains.

The reliability of these probability surfaces is substantiated by the calibration and validation behaviour of the national GWR model (Fig. 2). The model was trained on 25,232 balanced historical samples, including 12,628 collapse and 12,604 non-collapse observations, and raw GWR regression scores were transformed into bounded probabilities using robust sigmoid calibration. The calibrated probabilities ranged from 0.072 to 0.850 and showed strong separation between stable and collapse-prone settings, with 35.9% of samples assigned to the lowest susceptibility class and 34.8% assigned to the highest class after Jenks natural-break stratification. At the operating threshold of 0.500, the validation subset achieved sensitivity of 0.935, specificity of 0.926, accuracy of 0.930 and an F1-score of 0.931, indicating balanced detection of both collapse and non-collapse samples. Repeated stratified five-fold validation further confirmed that performance was not dependent on a single data partition, yielding test accuracy of 0.908 ± 0.004, balanced accuracy of 0.908 ± 0.004, F1-score of 0.910 ± 0.003, ROC-AUC of 0.968 ± 0.002 and average precision of 0.962 ± 0.003. The low Brier score (0.068 ± 0.002) and near one-to-one reliability curve demonstrate that the model retains meaningful probabilistic information. These diagnostics support the use of the calibrated GWR surface as a defensible basis for interpreting national sinkhole susceptibility patterns and for extending the analysis to future scenario projections.
